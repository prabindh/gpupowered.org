Rendering translucent materials like Glass, involves taking care of both reflection and refraction, to get realistic results. This also needs specific OpenGL functionality on render to texture, cube-maps, and different types of transformation and blending to get the final effect. Below is a summary of the components and techniques used for rendering materials like glass and water: OpenGL/GLSL API for reflect, refract functionality https://www.opengl.org/sdk/docs/man/html/reflect.xhtml Cube-maps are commonly used for obtaining a lookup of the environment for reflection, hence an introduction to the same: http://www3.di.uminho.pt/uce-cg/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/6-CubeMapping.pdf One of the earliest OpenGL (using fixed function) water shaders by Michael Horsch: http://blog.bonzaisoftware.com/tnp/gl-water-tutorial/ This has a good description of the workflow involved - normal terms, Fresnel factors, Reflection and Refraction. GPU Gems - Using Refraction and Normal maps by Tiago Sousa: This provides some more theoretical background on the effects involved. http://http.developer.nvidia.com/GPUGems2/gpugems2_chapter19.html Glass shader: Blender 2.7: Blender 2.74 (Turkish language) - https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AQq-ZTgdfsQ For this article, also refer to the background material on Blender shaders at, https://www.blender.org/manual/render/cycles/nodes/shaders.html OpenGL shader course by Villar: https://www.opengl.org/sdk/docs/tutorials/TyphoonLabs/Chapter_4.pdf GLSL Book chapter on Refraction and shaders: https://www.scss.tcd.ie/Michael.Manzke/CS7055/GLSL/GLSL-3rdEd-refraction.pdf Viktor Lidholt's presentation in GDC2015 on effects in 2D games, not just refraction, but a whole bunch of effects including particle systems: http://twvideo01.ubm-us.net/o1/vault/gdc2015/presentations/ViktorLidholt_Advanced_Visual_Effects_in_2D_Games.pdf Rendering glass with PoVRay: With global illumination, Photon mapping, Kd-trees, with lots of references http://www.cs.umd.edu/~mount/Indep/Alisa_Chen/caustics.html
